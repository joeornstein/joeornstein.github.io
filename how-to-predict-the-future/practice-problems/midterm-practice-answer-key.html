POLS 3220 Midterm Probability Practice

Answer Key

Problem 1

A basketball player with a 50% free throw shooting rate goes to the line to shoot 3 free throws. What’s the probability she makes 1 or fewer?

\(12.5\% \times 4 = 50\%\)

Problem 2

You’re volunteering with a phone bank for a political campaign. People only answer their phone 10% of the time. Out of the next 3 numbers on your call sheet, what’s the probability that anyone answers your call?

You could draw out the full tree here, but the most straightforward solution is to take advantage of the axioms of probability.

\(P(\text{anyone answers your call}) = 1 - P(\text{no one answers your calls})\)

The probability that no one answers a single call is 90%, so the probability that no one answers you three calls in a row is \(90\% \times 90\% \times 90\% \approx 73\%\).

Therefore, at least one person will pick up the phone with probability \(1 - 0.73\) or 27%.

Problem 3

The average movie director starts out with a 25% chance of making a Certified Fresh film. But that probability can go up or down over time. If the director makes a Certified Fresh film, he gets a boost of morale, and the probability increases by 10% for the next film he directs. Otherwise, he takes a hit to morale, and the probability decreases by 10% for the next film he directs. What’s the probability that a director gets at least 2 Certified Fresh films out of his first 3 films?

There are four outcomes on the tree where the director has 2/3 fresh films: terminal nodes 1, 2, 3, and 5. The probability of one of those four outcomes is:

\(3.9\% + 4.8\% + 4.1\% + 2.8\% = 15.6\%\)

Problem 4

You are trying to predict whether a bill will be passed by the US House of Representatives. Historically, only 10% of bills introduced in the House are passed. Among passed bills, 60% had bipartisan cosponsors. Among failed bills, only 15% had bipartisan cosponsors. The bill in question has bipartisan cosponsors. What is the probability that it passes?

\[ \text{Posterior Odds} = \text{Prior Odds} \times \text{Strength of Evidence} \]

  • Prior Odds = 1:9

  • Strength of Evidence = 4:1

  • Posterior Odds = 4:9

  • Posterior Probability = \(\frac{4}{13} \approx 31\%\)

Problem 5

Same question as above, but the bill does not have bipartisan cosponsors.

This one is quite tricky; in my original version of the answer key I got my own question wrong! Everything is the same except the Strength of Evidence. How much more likely is a bill to lack bipartisan cosponsors if it passes than if it fails? Well, among bills that passed, 40% lacked bipartisan cosponsors (100% minus the figure in Problem 4). Among bills that failed, 85% lacked bipartisan cosponsors. So:

\[ \text{Posterior Odds} = \text{Prior Odds} \times \text{Strength of Evidence} \]

  • Prior Odds = 1:9

  • Strength of Evidence = 40:85 = 8:17

  • Posterior Odds = 8:153

  • Posterior Probability = \(\frac{8}{161} \approx 5\%\)

Problem 6

Breast cancer is rare among women age 40-49; it only occurs in about 2% of women in this age range. A mammogram screening can help you determine if you have breast cancer, but the false positive rate is about 10% (i.e. 10% of women who don’t have breast cancer will receive a false positive result). The false negative rate is also around 10% (i.e. the mammogram will give a false negative in about 10% of women with breast cancer). If a woman in her 40s receives a positive result on a mammogram, what is the probability she has breast cancer?

The strength of evidence here is 9:1, because the probability of getting a positive mammogram is 90% if you do have breast cancer, and 10% if you don’t have breast cancer.

\[ \text{Posterior Odds} = \text{Prior Odds} \times \text{Strength of Evidence} \]

  • Prior Odds = 2:98 = 1:49

  • Strength of Evidence = 9:1

  • Posterior Odds = 9:49

  • Posterior Probability = \(\frac{9}{58} \approx 15.5\%\)
