Week 9: Urban Geometry — Zettelkasten Summary

Walkability as the core metric

Speck argues that walkability is the single best indicator of a city’s vitality — cities where basic needs are walkable are wealthier, healthier, and more pleasant. His four criteria: walking must be useful, safe, comfortable, and interesting. The punchiest formulation: city engineers “worshiping the twin gods of Smooth Traffic and Ample Parking” have made downtowns easy to reach but not worth arriving at.

Putnam’s finding reinforces the stakes: each additional 10 minutes of commute cuts community involvement by 10%. Auto-oriented suburbs significantly contributed to the broad-based decline in social trust.

The Suburban Experiment and auto-dependence

The 20th-century suburban build-out was an abrupt, top-down break from millennia of walkable development (Marohn, Strong Towns). Traditional development proceeds incrementally through trial and error, producing emergent design wisdom — shared walls that reduce construction and heating costs, buildings hugging the street that satisfy a deep psychological need to occupy edges of spaces.

Electric cars don’t solve auto-dependence. Tire particulates are now a larger source of pollution than tailpipe emissions.

Cars, geometry, and land use

A city is, almost by definition, a place where land is expensive. Cars are spectacularly space-inefficient in that context (see the classic Human Transit photo comparing transit modes). Transportation is fundamentally a land use problem: walk through any city and count the “missing teeth” — surface parking lots, dead frontages, land consumed by moving and storing cars rather than serving as places where people live.

The Fundamental Law of Road Congestion

Duranton and Turner find that vehicle kilometers traveled increase proportionately with highway capacity, meaning new roads don’t relieve congestion in equilibrium. This undermines the ASCE’s “infrastructure gap” calculations, which rely on the value of time saved — time that isn’t actually saved. Meanwhile, Anderson’s study of the 2003 LA transit strike found highway delays increased 47% when transit shut down, suggesting transit’s net benefits are larger than typically estimated.

The Density Paradox

There is a range of residential density (roughly, small-lot single-family homes) that is suboptimal for both cars and mass transit. Adding density worsens congestion, but the area is still too sparse to support good transit. This creates a “canyon in the fitness landscape” — communities in this zone may be the least likely to accept incremental densification.

Sprawl and externalities

The economist’s framing: sprawl is only inefficient if there are unpriced negative externalities — traffic congestion, carbon emissions, an amenity value for open space, or reduced social interactions. There is also a fiscal angle (Marohn): once public infrastructure is built out, adding residents just adds congestion, reversing the traditional development logic where more people meant more fiscal capacity.

Missing Middle and Jacobs

Zoning regulations that prohibit missing middle housing create monoculture neighborhoods — auto-dependent, low-density, single-use — lacking the diversity Jacobs identified as essential to thriving cities. The analogy to Scott’s Seeing Like a State is apt: just as scientific forestry replaced diverse forests with fragile monocultures, Euclidean zoning replaces diverse neighborhoods with fragile, car-dependent ones.

Street grids

Ellickson argues for the economic benefits of grid street layouts. A speculative framing: grids may maximize aggregate property values by optimizing the tradeoff between accessibility (more roads connecting more parcels) and the opportunity cost of land consumed by roads.

Human-scale design

Jacobs and Alexander (A Pattern Language) both observe that people naturally gravitate toward the edges of spaces. Traditional streetscapes provide these edges; suburban open spaces do not. The discomfort of wide-open suburban landscapes may be deeply rooted — we are, after all, the brains of a prey species.

Throughline: Designing cities around cars consumes expensive land inefficiently, induces demand rather than relieving congestion, erodes social capital, and produces monoculture neighborhoods that lack the diversity Jacobs identified as essential to urban vitality. Walkability is the corrective.
