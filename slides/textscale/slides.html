Measuring Latent Quantities from Text Embeddings

Joseph T. Ornstein

Department of Political Science, University of Georgia

Motivation

  • Our quantities of interest are often unobservable (latent).
  • Must be estimated based on observed behaviors.
    • e.g. ideology (Poole and Rosenthal 1985; Martin and Quinn 2002; Lowe et al. 2011; Bonica 2014)
    • e.g. human rights (Fariss 2014)
  • “Text-as-data” is one such approach to measuring latent quantities (Grimmer and Stewart 2013).
  • Past three years have seen a flurry of work applying LLMs to text-as-data tasks.
  • But LLMs rarely perform well “off-the-shelf” (Halterman and Keith 2025).
    • They’re built to be good at chatting! Not at measuring political science concepts.
  • In this talk, I’ll propose a principled approach to estimating latent quantities from text using LLM outputs.
  • Implemented in the new R package textscale.

Chatbots don’t work off-the-shelf

  • Le Mens and Gallego (2025) propose estimating ideology from text with an “ask-and-average” approach.

  • Does not yield a continuous scale with meaningful intervals.

  • Resulting measure too “lumpy” around multiples of 5 and 10.

  • Conflates ideology and partisanship (e.g. “Biden needs to RESIGN immediately and NEVER be seen in public again!” scores an 85).

Humans struggle with this too

  • There’s a reason we don’t ask human coders to annotate text this way!

  • Placing items on a continuous scale is a cognitively-demanding and poorly-defined task (Carlson and Montgomery 2017).

Pairwise Comparisons Framework

  • With a sufficiently large number of pairwise comparisons, we can estimate the underlying latent scale (Bradley and Terry 1952).

  • Consider a set of items indexed by \(i\), each possessing some latent quantity \(\theta_i\).

  • We’ll assume that annotators (human or chatbot) are good, but not perfect, at making pairwise comparisons.

    • As the distance between \(\theta_i\) and \(\theta_j\) increases, the odds an annotator chooses the correct item also increase.

  • Specifically, the key identification assumption of the Bradley-Terry model: \(\text{log-odds}(i > j) = \theta_i - \theta_j\)

    • Not just an arbitrary assumption for mathematical convenience!

    • Extensive literature in “psychophysics” (e.g. Thurstone 1927) demonstrates that our ability to perceive differences in stimuli depends on relative differences in magnitude, rather than absolute differences.

Pairwise Comparison Framework

  • It’s straightforward to apply this framework to coding political texts (Carlson and Montgomery 2017).

    • Crowd-sourcing a large number of these pairwise comparisons through online platforms like MTurk yields fast, reliable measures of latent quantities on a meaningfully continuous scale.

  • Of course, today these platforms are rife with “synthetic respondents” (Westwood 2025).

    • Reasonable to expect that >30% of crowd-work tasks performed by bots, including LLMs.

  • So should we just lean in, and let the chatbots do all the pairwise comparisons?

    • Wu et al. (2023) show that this is a promising approach.

    • ChatGPT is just as adept at pairwise comparisons as non-expert human coders.

  • But this approach has two major limitations, one practical and one principled.

Limitations of Pairwise Comparisons

  • First, the approach scales poorly. The number of pairwise comparisons increases quadratically with \(n\).

  • Exhaustive pairwise comparisons infeasible even for chatbots (in both time and cost) for the types of applications I’ll demonstrate here.

Limitations of Pairwise Comparisons

  • Second, using pairwise comparisons alone throws away some potentially useful information provided by the LLM.

  • Language models like ChatGPT encode text as real-valued vectors of numbers, called embeddings.

    • These embeddings are trained so that texts with similar meaning lie close together in vector space.
    • Somewhat famously, arithmetic operations on embeddings yield rudimentary analogies (Rodriguez and Spirling 2022).
      • \(\text{King} - \text{Man} + \text{Woman} \approx \text{Queen}\)
      • \(\text{France} - \text{Paris} + \text{Warsaw} \approx \text{Poland}\).
    • This suggests, somewhat loosely, that different dimensions in embedding space encode different concepts.

  • The approach I propose here uses pairwise comparisons to help identify which dimension in embedding space best captures the quantity of interest.

The Algorithm

Proceeds in six steps:

  1. Embeddings. For each document, retrieve an \(m\)-dimensional embedding representation. Yields an \(m \times n\) matrix denoted \(\mathbf{X}\).
    • In applications, I use 256-dimensional text embeddings from OpenAI.
  2. Partition. Split the documents into training and test sets.
    • In applications, I hold out 20% of documents for validation tests.
  3. Pairwise Comparisons. Select a random subset of possible document pairs. Annotators (human or chatbot) compare based on quantity of interest. \(Y_{ij} = 1\) if \(i\) is selected over \(j\) and \(Y_{ij} = 0\) otherwise.
  4. Fit Model. Estimate logistic regression coefficients \(\hat{\beta}\) predicting \(Y_{ij}\) from embedding differences \((X_i - X_j)\).
    • To avoid overfitting, I fit an L2-regularized logistic regression tuned by 10-fold cross-validation.

The Algorithm

  1. Validation. Ensure that the model accurately predicts pairwise comparisons in the held-out test set.
  2. Estimate Latent Quantities. \(\hat{\theta} = X\hat{\beta}\)

Applications

Applications

Three applications to document scaling tasks, measuring:

  1. Negativity in campaign advertisements
  2. Ideology expressed in US political party platforms (1992-2020)
  3. The strength of political arguments

Application 1: Ad Tone

  • Carlson and Montgomery (2017) measure the negativity of 935 televised Senate campaign ads from 9,528 crowd-sourced pairwise comparisons.

    • Given the date, we know these pairwise comparisons are coming from humans!

  • Can a logistic regression trained on text embeddings predict which ad crowd-coders will select as the most negative in tone?

    • Yes!!

Application 1: Ad Tone

Application 1: Ad Tone

  • Fitted values from embeddings predict expert classification slightly better than estimates from pairwise comparisons alone.

  • Highly scalable: can apply the fitted model to the text embedding of any new campaign advertisement.

Application 2: Ideology

  • The Manifestos Project measures ideology of political platforms by counting up the number of quasi-sentences coded as “left” or “right” (Lowe et al. 2011).

  • According to this measure, here’s how US political party platforms have changed over time:

Application 2: Ideology

  • What should we make of the Democratic Party’s swing to the left in recent years?

  • Is it an artifact of the way the MP measure is constructed?

  • The Democratic Party platform today has more sentences devoted to left-leaning policy ideas, reflecting a shift in emphasis.

  • But perhaps the sentences themselves are no more extreme.

  • In this application, I construct a sentence-level measure of ideology to assess how the party platforms have shifted since the 1990s.

Application 2: Ideology

  1. Embeddings. Retrieve embeddings for 18,310 sentences from Democratic and Republican Party platforms between 1992 and 2020.
    • I create “anonymized” versions of each sentence, removing party labels and politician names.
  2. Partition. Hold out a random set of sentences as a test set.
  3. Pairwise Comparison. Select a random subset of 40,000 sentence pairs, and ask GPT-5.2 “Which is further to the right politically?”
  4. Fit Model.
  5. Validate. Assess whether the model can accurately predict pairwise comparisons in the held-out test set.
  6. Estimate Latent Quantities. Project embeddings onto an ideology score and assess face validity.

Application 3: Argument Strength

  • No established method to measure the quality/strength of political arguments (Druckman 2022).

  • Naunov, Rueda-Cañòn, and Ryan (2025) ask survey respondents to write persuasive messages on three topics (illegal immigration, clean energy subsidies, and transgender rights).

    • \(n = 400\) arguments, roughly evenly split between topics and liberal/conservative position.

  • They then provide these arguments to a second set of respondents, and measure pre-post attitude change.

  • In this application, I construct a measure of “argument strength”, and test whether it correlates with actual attitude change in the experiment.

Application 3: Argument Strength

  1. Embeddings. For each argument I retrieve 256-dimensional embeddings from OpenAI.
  2. Partition. I hold out arguments about immigration as a test set.
  3. Pairwise Comparison. I select a random subset of 6,000 argument pairs, and ask GPT-5.1 “Which argument do you think is more likely to persuade a listener to change their position?”
    • To avoid inadvertently introducing algorithmic bias towards liberal or conservative arguments, I only request pairwise comparisons within categories (e.g. “liberal environment” or “conservative transgender”).
  4. Fit Model.
  5. Validate. Assess whether the model trained on arguments about the environment and transgender rights can accurately predict pairwise comparisons about immigration arguments.
  6. Estimate Latent Quantities. Assess whether estimated argument strength correlates with actual attitude change.

Application 3: Argument Strength

Application 3: Argument Strength

  • Cross-issue validation is poor: 55.8% accuracy (ICI = 0.26).

  • The model does not appear to generalize from environment/transgender arguments to immigration arguments.

  • Therefore, I would not recommend applying the model to arguments in a different domain without generating a new set of pairwise comparisons.

  • Nevertheless, face validity of the argument strength scores is reasonable:

    • The lowest-rated arguments are often poorly-written or employ ad hominem attacks (e.g. “senile Biden regime”).

    • The highest-rated arguments cite evidence or make a human connection (e.g. “I am the child of an immigrant”).

Wrapping Up

Wrapping Up

  • The proposed approach has a number of key advantages over existing methods.

    • Meaningful Continuous Measures. Generates scores where the units convey meaningful information about the position of a text on the latent dimension.

    • Confidence Intervals. 95% prediction intervals from the logistic regression provide a transparent summary of measurement error for each observation.

    • Highly Scalable. Can be applied to much larger text corpora than approaches that rely on pairwise comparisons alone.

    • Easy To Validate. Straightforward to perform validation tests at every stage of the text-to-measure pipeline (Park and Montgomery 2025).
